# Language Guidelines (Principles, Not Phrases)

**INSTRUCTION:** This reference describes TYPES of language moves. Do NOT copy specific phrases. Understand the principle, then create your own wording.

---

## Voice Principles

### Use First Person Plural
**Principle:** "We" creates partnership with reader, implies shared exploration.

**Instead of:** passive constructions, third person, "one should"
**Use:** active "we" constructions

---

### Be Direct, Not Evasive
**Principle:** State findings clearly. Add caveats as separate, specific statements.

**Weak pattern (avoid):**
```
It could potentially perhaps be suggested that under certain 
conditions there may be a tendency toward...
```

**Strong pattern (principle):**
- Main claim as direct statement
- Caveats as specific, separate qualifications
- "This suggests X. However, [specific limitation]."

---

### Acknowledge Uncertainty Explicitly
**Principle:** Credibility comes from honesty about what you don't know.

**Types of honest hedging:**
- Confidence intervals, not point estimates
- "suggests" vs "proves"
- Specific conditions under which findings hold
- Explicit limitations section

---

## Sentence-Level Principles

### The Setup-Payoff Structure
**Principle:** Create tension, then resolve it.

**Pattern:** [Current painful state] — but [transformation possible]

---

### The Reframe
**Principle:** Translate technical concept to accessible terms.

**Pattern:** After technical explanation, restate in everyday language.
Signals: "In other words...", "Put differently...", "This means..."

---

### The Concrete Example
**Principle:** Abstract claims need grounding in specifics.

**Pattern:** General statement → "For instance..." → specific case

---

### The Progressive Reveal
**Principle:** Build anticipation for complex or exciting content.

**Pattern:** Acknowledge you're warming up, signal more is coming.

---

## Paragraph-Level Principles

### Short Paragraphs
**Principle:** Dense paragraphs lose readers. 3-5 sentences max.

### One Idea Per Paragraph
**Principle:** Each paragraph has one job. State it, support it, move on.

### Transitions That Work
**Principle:** Reader should never wonder "why am I reading this now?"

**Transition types:**
- Pivot (problem → solution)
- Sequence (first → then → finally)
- Contrast (unlike X, this approach...)
- Consequence (therefore, this means...)

---

## Formatting Principles

### Bold for Introduction
**Principle:** First appearance of key term gets **bold**. Subsequent uses don't.

### Italics for Emphasis
**Principle:** *Italics* for emphasis, foreign terms, or meta-reference to words.

### Lists for Parallel Items
**Principle:** Use bullets/numbers when items are truly parallel.

| Use numbers when | Use bullets when |
|------------------|------------------|
| Order matters | Order doesn't matter |
| Sequence/steps | Parallel options |
| Referring back by number | Simple enumeration |

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### ❌ Buzzword Soup
**Signs:** "leverage", "synergize", "paradigm", "best-in-class", "transformative"
**Fix:** Plain language. What specifically happens?

### ❌ Passive Throughout  
**Signs:** "It was found that...", "The model was trained...", "Results were obtained..."
**Fix:** Active voice with clear subject.

### ❌ Infinite Hedging
**Signs:** "potentially", "perhaps", "somewhat", "in some cases", all in one sentence
**Fix:** One clear claim + one specific caveat.

### ❌ Jargon Without Definition
**Signs:** Acronyms and technical terms with no explanation
**Fix:** First use gets plain-language explanation or parenthetical.

### ❌ No Signposting
**Signs:** Reader lost in middle of post, unclear why sections exist
**Fix:** Headers that preview content; transitions that connect sections.

---

## Section-Specific Guidelines

### Opening
- First sentence: must hook (question, pain, counterintuitive claim)
- No definitions first
- Stakes must be clear within 3 sentences

### Technical Content
- Intuition before math
- Visual before formula
- Interpretation after equation

### Conclusions
- Synthesize, don't summarize
- Forward-looking final statement
- No new information

---

## Final Instruction

When writing:
1. Apply these PRINCIPLES to your content
2. Create your own phrasing that embodies the principle
3. Check your draft against the anti-patterns
4. If you catch yourself copying phrases from here, rephrase
